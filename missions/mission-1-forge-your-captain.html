See Your Captain

Before you generate anything, describe Luffy to Claude Code. Not the anime Luffy — YOUR Luffy. The one who stands on the deck of the Going Merry in Pirate Gate. Claude will ask you questions to sharpen the vision.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
I'm designing Monkey D. Luffy for my game Pirate Gate. Let me describe what I see.
Then describe him. Think about: which Luffy (pre-timeskip? post-timeskip? your own version?), the straw hat, the outfit, the stance, the expression, the scars.

Claude will ask follow-up questions — go deep. The more specific you are now, the better the concept art will be.

  • 🍱 Which era? East Blue Luffy with the simple red vest? Post-timeskip with the chest scar and open shirt? Something you invent?
  • 🎩 The Straw Hat matters. It's the most iconic item in One Piece. How does he wear it in your game? On his head? Around his neck? Tilted?
  • 💪 Luffy is rubber. Does his stance show it? Loose, flexible, unpredictable? Or is he standing like a traditional fighter with fists up?
  1. Open Claude Code. Describe YOUR Luffy — outfit, stance, expression, details.
  2. Answer Claude's follow-up questions. Go specific.
  3. When Claude says it has a clear picture — move to Phase 2.

Phase 02 — Concept Art
02
Create
Bring Him to Sight
15 min
›

Now Claude generates concept art based on your description. You'll see Luffy for the first time — YOUR Luffy. Then you iterate. The first image is never the final image.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
Generate concept art of Luffy based on what I described. Full body, anime cel-shaded style, clean background. I need to see the straw hat, the outfit, and the stance clearly.

When the image appears — look at it carefully. What's right? What needs to change?

🔁
The iteration loop. Tell Claude what to keep and what to fix: "The straw hat is right but the vest should be more red. Keep the proportions." Usually takes 2–4 rounds to land on the one that makes you say "that's him."
🎨
For the 3D step: The final image needs to show Luffy clearly — full body, readable angle (front or 3/4 view), details visible. If the image is too stylized or abstract, Meshy will struggle to turn it into a 3D model. Clean shapes, clear silhouette, anime style with defined outlines.
  1. Tell Claude to generate the concept art.
  2. Look at the result. Say what's right and what needs to change.
  3. Iterate. Each round gets closer.
  4. When you see YOUR Luffy — when the image matches what's in your head — lock it in.

Phase 03 — The Forge
03
Build
Into the Third Dimension
10 min
›

You don't need to leave Claude Code. Claude sends your concept art directly to Meshy and builds the 3D model for you. Same Meshy you know — but now Claude calls it, so everything stays in one conversation.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
Turn my Luffy concept art into a 3D model. Use Image to 3D.
Claude will send the image to Meshy via MCP and show you the result when it's ready. Takes about a minute.

When the model comes back — review it. Does it capture the straw hat? The vest? The overall feel of YOUR Luffy? If not, tell Claude what's wrong and try again.

  1. Tell Claude to convert your concept art to 3D.
  2. Wait about a minute. Claude will tell you when it's done.
  3. Review the 3D model. Does it look like your Luffy?
  4. If not — say what's wrong and generate again. Iteration is your workflow.
  5. Lock in the best result.
💡
Don't chase perfection. The 3D model won't be a pixel-perfect copy of the concept art. The goal is a recognizable Luffy with the right energy — the straw hat, the stance, the vibe. When we load him into the game engine with cel-shading, he'll look like an anime character.

Phase 04 — The Dojo

Teach Him to Fight

Your Luffy model is static — a rubber statue. Now you give him bones and teach him to fight. Claude handles the rigging and animation through Meshy — you stay in the conversation and choose the moves.

💬 Say this to Claude Code
Rig Luffy as a humanoid so he can move. Then let's pick his combat animations.
Claude will auto-rig the model through Meshy MCP. Then you choose which fighting moves to give him.

The design decisions are yours. Claude will present the animation options. You pick which ones become Luffy's moveset in Pirate Gate. Each animation you choose is a move in your captain's arsenal.

💪
This is the minimum, not the limit. If you find a combat move in the library that feels right for Luffy — add it. Look for rapid punches (Gum Gum Gatling base), charged punches (Gum Gum Bazooka base), or spinning attacks (Gum Gum Whip). You're the game designer.

Export: Once you've chosen all the moves, tell Claude to download the final animated model as FBX. One file with Luffy's model, skeleton, and every animation you selected.

🏆
Mission complete. That FBX file is the first artifact of Pirate Gate: One Piece. YOUR Luffy — designed from concept art you directed, brought into 3D, rigged, and animated with a full combat moveset. In the next session, we build his world.
  1. Tell Claude to rig Luffy. Claude handles it through Meshy — takes about 30 seconds.
  2. Claude presents the animation options. Pick Luffy's combat moveset.
  3. Select at minimum the animations in the table above.
  4. Ask Claude about extras. Rapid punches? Charged attacks? Kicks?
  5. Tell Claude to download the final animated model as FBX.
  6. Done. That file is Monkey D. Luffy.
